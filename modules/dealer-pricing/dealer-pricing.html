Dealer & Wholesale Pricing

Dealers and wholesale buyers don't pay retail. A price list is a stack of RULES the pricing engine walks at add-to-cart time — cost-plus markup, %-off-list tiers, volume brackets — resolved deterministically by scope and quantity. This guide builds the classic dealer rule: Cost Price + 30%, the same 'landed cost plus margin' every distributor uses, with the account's own cost as the base so the margin holds even as purchase prices move.

STEP 01·NAVIGATE

Open Price Lists

A deployment can run many price lists — retail, a channel-specific list, one per dealer tier. Each is a named container of rules with a currency and an active flag. Products are shared company-wide; the price a given buyer sees is whichever list resolves for them, so the list is the pricing dimension, not the product.

2

STEP 02·CLICK

Create the dealer price list

Name it for the segment it serves — 'Dealer / Wholesale — Cost Plus'. Currency is the tenant default. Leave Channel unset to apply it across every sales surface, or scope it to one channel (POS, a specific storefront) when only that surface should see dealer prices. The list is empty until you add rules.

3

STEP 03·CHOOSE

Add a Cost-Plus rule

Set Computation to Formula and Base to Cost Price — this is what makes it cost-PLUS: the rule builds the price up from what YOU paid, not down from a retail sticker. Base 'Cost Price' reads the product's own cost, so the dealer margin holds automatically when purchase prices change. 'Markup % over base' is the headline field: enter 30 and the engine computes cost × 1.30. Markup applies first (builds the price); an optional Discount % then trims it, a Surcharge adds a flat handling fee, and Round-to-nearest tidies the result.

4

STEP 04·RESOLVE

How the engine resolves a dealer price

At add-to-cart the pricing engine finds every rule that applies (by scope and quantity), sorts them — scope rank, then higher min-quantity, then priority ascending (LOWER priority runs first) — and applies the winner. For a product costing 10,000 (minor units), this rule returns 13,000: cost × (1 + 30/100). Add a second rule with a higher Min Qty and its own markup to reward volume; the bracket with the highest satisfied Min Qty wins. The whole resolution is in-process — pricing never makes an HTTP hop on the hot path.
