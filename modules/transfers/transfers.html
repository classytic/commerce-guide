Inter-branch Transfers

A transfer is the only operation that touches TWO branches at once — and it exposes the whole architecture. Because Flow is per-branch isolated (organizationId = branchId), moving stock needs DUAL contexts: one to decrement the sender, another to increment the receiver. And because accounting is a SINGLE company ledger with branch as a dimension, the same movement posts a PAIR of journal entries bridged by an in-transit clearing account — company inventory never changes, only the branch tag does. Follow one challan from dispatch to receive.

1

STEP 01·NAVIGATE

Transfers are challans with a route

Every transfer is a challan (delivery note) with a Route — a sender branch and a receiver branch — moving through create → approve → dispatch → receive. The route is the whole point: unlike a purchase (goods from a supplier) or a sale (goods to a customer), a transfer moves goods between two branches you own, so both sides of your own books must move.

2

STEP 02·CLICK

Open the challan — approval gates dispatch

The detail shows the route, the line items, and who approved / dispatched / received it with timestamps. Approval is a hard gate: an incomplete approval chain returns 422 and dispatch is blocked. Nothing physical or financial has moved yet — approval only authorises the dispatch, exactly as a PO approval authorises receiving.

3

STEP 03·RESOLVE

Dispatch resolves the SENDER's stock (context 1)

Dispatch runs in the SENDER's Flow context. An outbound moveGroup takes the units out of the sender's stock location into transit, and the sender's quant decrements. The goods have physically left but not yet arrived — this is the in-transit state. Note the actor + timestamp: DISPATCHED BY / DISPATCHED AT are stamped here.

4

STEP 04·RESOLVE

Receive resolves the RECEIVER's stock (context 2)

When the goods arrive, receive runs in the RECEIVER's Flow context — a second, separate context. An inbound moveGroup lands the units in the receiver's stock location and increments its quant. Why two contexts and not one? Because each branch is an isolated stock ledger (organizationId = branchId); you literally cannot touch the receiver's quant from the sender's scope. The status history records the full progression: Approved → Dispatched → Partial Received → Received.

5

STEP 05·RESOLVE

The same two steps post a PAIR of ledger entries

Each leg posts one JE, tagged with that leg's branch. DISPATCH (sender tag): Dr Inventory-in-Transit (1179) / Cr Merchandise (1164). RECEIVE (receiver tag): Dr Merchandise (1164) / Cr Inventory-in-Transit (1179). The 1179 clearing account holds the in-flight value between the two legs — that is what decouples them in time. Net company inventory is ZERO; only the per-branch DIMENSION shifted. This is the concrete reason the platform runs ONE ledger with a branch dimension rather than separate per-branch books: a transfer is not a sale, it just re-tags an asset from one branch to another.

6

STEP 06·RESOLVE

Freight capitalizes; a cancel reverses cleanly

If the transfer carries freight, that transit cost is capitalized into the receiver's inventory (IAS 2): Receive debits 1164 for goods + transit and credits 2126 Transfer Cost Clearing, which the host later clears against the carrier's actual invoice (Dr 2126 / Cr A/P). And if a dispatched transfer is cancelled, the system does not erase the posted entries — accounting convention is that posted JEs are immutable — it posts NEW reversing entries so both branches' books unwind, with the audit trail showing the original and its reversal side by side.
